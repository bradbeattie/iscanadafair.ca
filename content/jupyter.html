Exploring the disproportionality of Canadian parties¶

I've generated a database of Canadian elections, and I'm curious if there's anything in here that might be of relevance to the discussion of disproportionality.

from IPython.display import display

# Read in dataframe
df1 = pd.read_csv("lop.parl.gc.ca_elections_per_riding/summary.tsv", sep="\t", comment="#")
df2 = pd.read_csv("history-per-year.tsv", sep="\t", comment="#", index_col="Election")
df2["Date"] = pd.to_datetime(df2["Date"])
df1["Date"] = df1["Election"].map(df2["Date"])
df1["Seat %"] = df1["Seats"] / df1.groupby(['Election'])['Seats'].transform(sum)
df1["Vote %"] = df1["Votes"] / df1.groupby(['Election'])['Votes'].transform(sum)
party_colors = pd.read_csv("party-colors.tsv", sep="\t", index_col="Party", comment="~")

# Augment data df1 (Elections by year and party)
df1["Year"] = df1["Date"].map(lambda x: x.year)
df1["Seat % - Vote %"] = df1["Seat %"] - df1["Vote %"]
df1["Seat % / Vote %"] = df1["Seat %"] / df1["Vote %"]
df1["Grouped Color"] = df1["Party"].map(party_colors["Color"]).fillna("grey")
df1n = df1[(df1["Party"] != "Independent") & (df1["Votes"] > 0) & (df1["Seats"] > 0)]

# Augment df2 (Elections by year) with matching df1 data (winning party)
# http://www.elections.ca/content.aspx?section=ele&dir=turn&document=index&lang=e#ftn2
df2["Electors / Population"] = df2["Electors"] / df2["Population"]
df2["Ballots / Population"] = df2["Ballots"] / df2["Population"]
df2["Ballots / Electors"] = df2["Ballots"] / df2["Electors"]
max_seats_per_date = df1.groupby(['Date'])['Seats'].transform(max)
df2 = pd.merge(df2, df1[max_seats_per_date == df1['Seats']], on="Date")

Proportionality indexed on popular vote¶

If our electoral system was proportional, we'd expect the following to roughly generate a horizontal line with some fuzziness near low popular votes due to rounding of seat assignment. But given Canada's use of FPTP and the Cube Rule, we expect to see significant distortion as a party gains more votes.

fig, (ax1, ax2) = plt.subplots(ncols=2)
plt.subplots_adjust(left=-1)
sns.regplot(x="Vote %", y="Seat % - Vote %", data=df1n, order=2, ax=ax1)
sns.regplot(x="Vote %", y="Seat % / Vote %", data=df1n, order=2, ax=ax2)
ax1.add_patch(patches.Rectangle((-1, -1), 2, 1, alpha=0.05, facecolor="black"))
ax2.add_patch(patches.Rectangle((-1, -1), 2, 2, alpha=0.05, facecolor="black"))
ax1.set_xticklabels(['{:3.0f}%'.format(x * 100) for x in ax1.get_xticks()])
ax2.set_xticklabels(['{:3.0f}%'.format(x * 100) for x in ax2.get_xticks()])
ax1.set_yticklabels(['{:3.0f}%'.format(y * 100) for y in ax1.get_yticks()])
ax2.set_yticklabels(['{:3.1f}x'.format(y) for y in ax2.get_yticks()]);

It's interesting to note the non-linear relation here. Surprisingly, parties with a very low popular vote are better represented than parties with a moderately low popular vote.

Proportionality indexed on popular vote and party¶

FPTP tends to favour parties that cluster their support in a smaller number of ridings than parties that spread their support across all ridings. So we'd expect to see unusually high proportionality for the Bloc with respect to their overall popular vote.

Proportionality indexed on era¶

Duverger's Law speaks of a pressure that FPTP exterts on parties. While Canada has yet to collapse into a two-party country, I wonder if there's a change in disproportionality over time.

In [4]:
first_year = int(df1["Year"].head(1))
last_year = int(df1["Year"].tail(1))
eras = 5
era_years = math.ceil((last_year - first_year + 1) / eras)
df1["Era"] = df1["Year"].map(lambda x: "{} - {}".format(
    (x - first_year) // era_years * era_years + first_year,
    (x - first_year) // era_years * era_years + first_year + era_years - 1,
))
g = sns.lmplot(x="Vote %", y="Seat % - Vote %", hue="Era", data=df1, order=2)
g.add_legend()
g.set(ylim=(-0.2, 0.3))
g.set(xlim=(0.0, None))
for axlist in g.axes:
    for ax in axlist:
        ax.set_xticklabels(['{:3.0f}%'.format(x * 100) for x in ax.get_xticks()])
        ax.set_yticklabels(['{:3.0f}%'.format(y * 100) for y in ax.get_yticks()])
        ax.add_patch(patches.Rectangle((-1, -1), 2, 1, alpha=0.07, facecolor="black"))

Tough to say for sure, but the second-order regression certainly hints at an increase in disproportionality.

Voter turnout¶

It's worth noting the the percentage of eligible voters has drastically increased since confederation (see Election Canada's "A History of the Vote in Canada"), most notably in 1920 with The Dominion Elections Act.

Similarity between the past two elections¶

It's worth noting the Liberal platform's inclusion of electoral reform while they were significantly underrepresented, and their breaking of that platform policy after winning a significant over representation.
